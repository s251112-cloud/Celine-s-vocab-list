## 🎭 Essential Idioms Bank (For Tone & Narrative Variance)

These idiomatic phrases should be deployed strategically in **informal or semi-formal genres** (e.g., blog entries, feature articles, or letters to friends) to demonstrate native-like expression.

* **Bite the bullet:** To confront an unavoidable, highly difficult situation with grit and resolve.
    * *Usage:* "The deadline is tonight, so I must **bite the bullet** and finish the script."
* **Burn the midnight oil:** To stay awake late into the night working or studying intensely.
    * *Usage:* "Countless candidates **burn the midnight oil** during the mock exam period."
* **Cost an arm and a leg:** Extremely expensive; requiring excessive financial sacrifice.
    * *Usage:* "Purchasing premium simulation software can **cost an arm and a leg** for local startups."
* **Cut corners:** To perform an assignment poorly or skip steps to save immediate time or money.
    * *Usage:* "Never **cut corners** when conducting safety checks on building structures."
* **Hit the nail on the head:** To describe exactly what is causing a particular problem or situation.
    * *Usage:* "Your editorial completely **hit the nail on the head** regarding student stress factors."
* **Once in a blue moon:** An event or phenomenon that occurs very rarely or infrequently.
    * *Usage:* "Due to their busy shifts, the siblings see each other only **once in a blue moon**."
* **Spill the beans:** To accidentally or prematurely reveal confidential data or a secret.
    * *Usage:* "The marketing team accidentally **spilled the beans** about the new smartphone release."
* **Under the weather:** Feeling slightly unwell, fatigued, indisposed, or minorly sick.
    * *Usage:* "The team captain missed the morning briefing because he felt a bit **under the weather**."

## 🏃‍♂️ Essential Phrasal Verbs Matrix (Grammatical Connectors)

Phrasal verbs combine a base verb with a particle, altering the core definition. They are highly tested elements in Paper 3 Integrated Tasks.

* **Bring up:** To introduce a specific topic or argument during a conversation, debate, or text.
    * *Usage:* "We must **bring up** the issue of recycling containers at the next community meeting."
* **Call off:** To officially cancel an event, action, or operation that was previously arranged.
    * *Usage:* "The outdoor concert was **called off** due to an adverse amber rainstorm warning."
* **Carry on:** To persist or continue with an action or task despite encountering obstacles.
    * *Usage:* "The research team decided to **carry on** with the trial despite the preliminary setback."
* **Find out:** To successfully discover or learn a new fact or piece of data for the first time.
    * *Usage:* "Our objective is to **find out** what triggers structural drops in user retention."
* **Get along (with):** To maintain a friendly, cooperative, and smooth relationship with someone.
    * *Usage:* "It is vital for student leaders to **get along with** team members across all cohorts."
* **Give up:** To cease making an effort; to stop a particular habit, trial, or pursuit permanently.
    * *Usage:* "No matter how dense the reading text is, do not **give up** on analyzing the context clues."
* **Look up to:** To hold someone in profound respect and view them as an influential role model.
    * *Usage:* "Many junior debaters deeply **look up to** their senior graduation coaches."
* **Put off:** To postpone, delay, or defer executing an action or assignment until a later timeline.
    * *Usage:* "Procrastinating students often **put off** their assignment completion until the final night."
* **Run out of:** To completely deplete the available supply of a specific resource or item.
    * *Usage:* "The medical center is about to completely **run out of** basic testing kits."
* **Turn down:** To formally reject, decline, or refuse an invitation, offer, or structural application.
    * *Usage:* "The applicant decided to **turn down** the scholarship offer due to family commitments."
